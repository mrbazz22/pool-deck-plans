🏖️ What This Is

A concave curved transition deck that hugs the pool edge like a beach entry. It sits just below the main deck — a gentle step down — and gives the dogs (and people) an easy, low-profile surface near the water.

Completely freestanding. Not attached to the main deck. Built on Eurotec pedestals with its own frame, joists, and decking. ~1" gap between this section and the main deck lets both move independently.

Height: ~5" above coping (main deck is ~7") · Style: Modern rustic · Stain: BEHR Stonehedge to match

01Overview & Design Intent

This is a separate build — freestanding, lighter duty, and shaped by the pool.

  • Shape: Concave curve on the back edge following a 24' diameter round pool. Straight front edge meets the main deck. Straight sides on left and right.
  • Purpose: "Beach entry" transition — lower than the main deck, close to coping level. The dogs will love having a wide, stable surface to lounge on right next to the water.
  • Independence: This frame does NOT attach to the main deck. A ~1" gap between the two frames allows for thermal expansion, ground movement, and independent settling.
  • Framing: 2×4 joists (lighter than the main deck's 2×6) at 12" O.C. — closer spacing compensates for the smaller lumber.
  • Decking: 2×6 boards (actual 1.5" × 5.5") — heavier and more stable than 5/4×6, better for a low-profile section that may see wet paws.
  • Height difference: Main deck is ~7" above coping. This section is ~5" above coping — a ~2" step down from the main deck.
💡 Why 2×4 Joists Here? Shorter spans (~3' to ~5') mean 2×4 is plenty strong. Using 2×4 keeps the profile lower and uses less lumber. The trade-off is closer joist spacing — 12" O.C. instead of 16".

02Framing Plan

Concave back edge · Straight front edge · Straight sides · Joists run front-to-back toward the curve.

Perimeter

  • Front edge (meets main deck): Straight, doubled 2×4 rim joist. Butts against (but does not attach to) the main deck's back rim. Maintains ~1" gap. Height is ~2" lower than main deck surface.
  • Back edge (pool side): Concave curve following the pool. Doubled 2×4 rim joists cut to a smooth arc. This is the signature visual element.
  • Side edges (left/right): Straight, doubled 2×4 rim joists connecting the front and back edges. Vary in length from ~3' on the center to ~5' at the outer ends.

Joists

  • Direction: Front-to-back — perpendicular to the straight front edge, pointing toward the curved back edge.
  • Spacing: 12" O.C. (closer than main deck's 16" O.C. because 2×4 spans less).
  • Lengths: Vary from ~3' at center to ~5' at the sides. Each joist is cut to butt into the curved back rim at the correct angle.
  • Ends: Face-screwed to front and back rim joists with 3" exterior screws — 2 per end.

04The Curve

The concave back rim is the visual centerpiece. Here's how to mark and cut it accurately.

Finding the Arc

  • Pool center is already known from the main deck stakeout. Use the same center point.
  • Radius: 12' from pool center (half of 24' diameter). The pool coping sits at this radius.
  • Back edge of this deck section: Follows the same 12' radius arc — the deck back rim hugs the pool edge.
  • Mark it on the ground: Drive a stake at pool center. Tie a string to it at exactly 12' length. Swing the string in an arc across the build area. Mark the arc on the ground with spray paint or flour.
  • Transfer to lumber: Lay out doubled 2×4s for the back rim. Use the stringline again to transfer the arc onto the boards, or use a flexible batten (thin strip of wood or PVC) bent to match the ground marks.

Cutting the Curved Rim

  • Doubled 2×4s: Cut both layers of the back rim together so they match exactly. Stack them, clamp them, trace the arc on top, and cut through both at once.
  • Tools: Bandsaw gives the cleanest curve. Jigsaw with a fine-tooth blade works too — go slow and stay outside your line, then sand to the line.
  • Length: The back rim spans ~20' of arc. If your 2×4 stock is 8' or 10', you'll need to splice pieces with angled scarf joints or simple overlaps. Stagger splices between the two layers.

Field Joists Meet the Curve

  • Each field joist butts into the curved back rim at a slightly different angle.
  • At center: Joist is nearly perpendicular to the curve — square cut is fine.
  • At sides: Joist meets the curve at an angle — cut the joist end at a slight bevel so it sits flush against the rim.
  • Tip: Dry-fit each joist, mark the angle directly against the rim, then cut. Don't try to precut all angles — the curve is subtle and it's easy to be off by a degree.
💡 Flexible Batten Trick A ¼" × 1" strip of pine or a piece of PVC trim makes a perfect flexible batten. Nail or clamp it at the endpoints and at the center of your arc, let it flex naturally into a fair curve, then trace along it. It'll give you a smoother curve than a string or a freehand line.

05Decking

2×6 boards run left→right. The last row follows the curve with angled end cuts.

  • Direction: Boards run left→right, parallel to the straight front edge and perpendicular to the joists.
  • Gaps: ¼" between boards. 2×6 moves more than 5/4×6 — especially with dark stain in sun — so err slightly wider.
  • Lengths: Most boards are full-length left→right across the ~20' width, butted end-to-end over joists if needed (2×6 butt joints are stronger than 5/4). Near the back edge, boards are cut to varying lengths to follow the concave curve.
  • Back edge detail: The last row of decking follows the curve. Each board end is cut at a slight angle so the row traces the arc cleanly. No single board follows the full curve — instead, a series of short angled cuts create a segmented curve that looks smooth from a few feet back.
  • Picture-frame option: You can run a border board perpendicular around the front and side edges for a finished look, or just let the clean ends of the left→right boards overhang slightly and bevel-cut them. The back edge against the pool coping is typically left as the curved row — no border needed there.
💡 Segmented Curve Looks Fine From 5 feet away, a series of angled 2×6 end cuts reads as a smooth curve. You don't need to bandsaw the decking. Cut each end at the angle where it meets the arc, keep gaps consistent, and it'll look intentional.

06Pedestal Layout

More pedestals per square foot than the main deck — 2×4 framing needs the support, and ground slopes toward the pool.

  • Front edge (near main deck): Lower to the ground. Use ECO M (1.5"–3") here. The frame sits close to grade.
  • Back edge (near pool): Ground typically slopes toward the pool. Use ECO L (3"–5.5") to compensate. Back pedestals will be noticeably taller.
  • Layout: Rows follow the curve. Place pedestals in a grid roughly 16" O.C. in both directions — closer than the main deck's spacing.
  • Under rims: Extra pedestals under all rim joists, especially at the curved back edge where the doubled 2×4 carries load from multiple joist ends.
  • Mid-span: One pedestal under each field joist near mid-span if the joist is longer than ~4'.

⚠️ Slope Away from Pool Even though the ground slopes toward the pool, the deck surface must slope slightly away from the pool for drainage. Aim for ⅛" per foot fall toward the front edge (away from the water). Adjust pedestal heights accordingly — back pedestals aren't just taller, they're also set slightly lower relative to the front to create that slope.

07Build Steps

Build this section after the main deck is framed but before final decking — or after the main deck is complete. Either way, keep it independent.

  1. Stake out the curve. Mark pool center, swing 12' string arc, spray-paint the back edge curve on the ground. Mark straight front edge parallel to main deck back rim, ~1" away. Mark side edges connecting front to back.
  2. Lay pedestal rows. Place ECO M/L pedestals in a grid following the curve. Start at front edge, work back. Adjust heights for level frame AND slope away from pool. Use a long level and stringline.
  3. Build front rim (straight). Doubled 2×4, ~20' long. Place on pedestals, level, check for straight. This is your reference edge — everything else builds from here.
  4. Build curved back rim. Cut doubled 2×4s to the marked arc. Splice if needed. Place on pedestals at back. Verify the curve is fair and the width matches your plan (~3' at center, ~5' at sides).
  5. Install side rims. Straight doubled 2×4s connecting front to back. Cut to length. Verify the frame is square-ish (diagonals roughly equal) and the front edge is parallel to the main deck.
  6. Install field joists at 12" O.C. Cut to varying lengths. Butt into curved back rim with angle cuts as needed. Face-screw to front and back rims. Verify each joist sits firmly on its pedestals.
  7. Install mid-span blocking. Short 2×4 pieces between joists where needed — especially near the curved back rim where joist ends converge. Prevents twist and adds stiffness.
  8. Apply joist tape. Butyl tape on all joist tops and rim tops before decking. Pay extra attention to the curved back rim — water tends to collect near the pool edge.
  9. Deck the frame. Lay 2×6 boards left→right. Start at front edge, work toward the curve. Maintain ¼" gaps. Cut last row to follow the curve with angled end cuts.
  10. Stain to match main deck. Touch up all cut ends with Stonehedge. If boards were pre-stained, end grain still needs a coat after cutting. Dark stain hides touch-ups well.

08Key Notes / Warnings

⚠️ Keep ~1" Gap from Main Deck This frame must remain independent. Do NOT screw, bolt, or bracket it to the main deck. Thermal expansion, ground settling, and seasonal movement will tear apart any rigid connection. A 1" gap is plenty — it'll disappear visually once both decks are stained the same color.
⚠️ 2×4 Joists Need 12" O.C. Spacing Do NOT use 16" O.C. with 2×4 joists. The span is short but the lumber is smaller. 12" O.C. is non-negotiable for stiffness and to prevent bounce — especially with dogs running on it.
⚠️ 2×6 Decking Is Heavy 2×6 weighs more than 5/4×6. The pedestal grid needs to be tighter — 16" O.C. in both directions, not the 2–3 per joist you'd use on the main deck. Undersupporting heavy decking leads to sag over time.
⚠️ Ground Slopes Toward Pool Back pedestals will be significantly taller than front pedestals. Buy more ECO L (3"–5.5") than you think you need. If a pedestal maxes out and you still need more height, shim with flat stone or composite shims under the pedestal base.
⚠️ Water Runoff: Slope Away from Pool The deck surface must shed water away from the pool, not toward it. Aim for ⅛" per foot fall toward the front edge. The pedestal height adjustments are what create this slope — the frame itself can be dead level front-to-back if the pedestals are graded correctly.
💡 Pre-Stain What You Can Stain the 2×4 framing and 2×6 decking boards on sawhorses before cutting, same as the main deck. The curved cuts will expose fresh end grain — touch those up on-site with a brush and a small cup of stain.
💡 The Dogs Will Approve This low, wide surface right at pool level is exactly where dogs want to be. Stable footing, easy in/out of the water, and close enough to supervise the humans. Build it strong.
