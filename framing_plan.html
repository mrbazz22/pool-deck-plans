All four rim joists are doubled 2×6 (two boards face-screwed together). This makes the frame beefier, gives the border sleeper something solid to anchor to, and matches the substantial look of the railings. You have the lumber to spare.

💡 The Big Win: Zero Joist Cuts
All 11 joist pieces (2 rims + 9 field) are used at their full stock length. No waste, no measuring mistakes, no crooked cuts. The only cut is the back rim (13' from 14'). Your ~11.5' pieces were made for this.
💡 Joist Spacing Math
13' = 156". At 16" O.C. with rim on both sides: joists at 0, 16, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96, 112, 128, 144, and rim at 156". That's 11 joists total (9 field + 2 rim). Last bay (144" to 156") is 12" — totally fine.

Blocking Plan

Blocking runs perpendicular to joists (left→right), at mid-span (~5.75' from back edge). This prevents the 11.5' joists from twisting and adds stiffness.

  • One row of blocking at ~5.75' from back edge
  • 10 pieces total (one per bay between joists)
  • 9 pieces: ~14.5" long (16" bay minus 1.5" joist width)
  • 1 piece: ~10.5" long (12" edge bay minus 1.5" joist width)
  • Cut from the 1' back rim offcut + any 14' offcuts you have
  • Face-screw to joists with 2.5" exterior screws
⚠️ Ledger Support
The 2×4 ledger (13') needs solid backing where the 2×4 curved joists attach. Screw the ledger to the ends of the 2×6 field joists AND add short 2×6 blocking pieces between joist ends where the ledger spans. Use 3" screws, 2 per connection.

025/4 + 2×6 Decking Layout — The 13' Solution

Your boards are 12'. Your deck is 13' wide. Here's the cleanest way to handle it without waste or ugly butt joints in the walking area.

How it works:

  1. Frame a 1' wide "landing strip" along the BACK edge (the edge away from the pool, where the box step will be)
  2. The main field is 12' wide — covered perfectly by your 12' boards running left→right
  3. The 1' strip is filled with shorter pieces butt-joined to the 12' boards
  4. The butt joint sits over the back rim joist + a piece of 2×6 blocking for extra support
  5. Because it's against the back edge under the step, it's barely visible and never walked on directly

💡 Decking Row Count Math
Main deck is 11.5' deep = 138". Each 5/4×6 board is ~5.5" wide + ¼" gap = 5.75" effective. 138" ÷ 5.75" = 24 rows. You can adjust the gap slightly (⅛"–⅜") to make the last row land clean on the rim without ripping a board.
💡 Curved Section Decking
Curved section is ~6.5' deep = 42". Boards run perpendicular (front-to-back), so they're cut to ~2–4' lengths. At ~5.75" effective width, 42" ÷ 5.75" = 7.3 → ~8 rows. Each piece is short, so one 12' board yields 3 pieces. About 3 boards cover the curved section.

Option B: The "Break Line" Border (Fancy)

Instead of butt-joining 1' pieces running the same direction, frame a perpendicular border using 1' long decking pieces running front-to-back (same direction as the joists). This looks intentional — like a picture frame or an inlaid border:

How it works:

  1. Frame a 1' wide border along the BACK edge (away from pool, where the box step will be)
  2. The border pieces run front-to-back (perpendicular to the main field boards)
  3. Each piece is ~1' long × 5.5" wide, running across the 1' strip depth
  4. You need ~24 of them (one per row)
  5. They sit on the doubled back rim + a 2×6 "sleeper" board running left→right for support
  6. The perpendicular direction creates a visual border — like an accent stripe
✅ SELECTED: Break Line Border (Option B)
Perpendicular border looks intentional — like you planned the direction change. It frames the main field and draws the eye. Doubled 2×6 rims make it even beefier.

⚠️ Support the Butt Joints — BOTH Options
Whether you use the landing strip or break line method, every butt joint needs solid support underneath. Screw a 2×6 or 2×4 "sleeper" block between joists directly under each joint. Pre-drill to prevent splitting. On the break line option, the sleeper also provides a nailing surface for the perpendicular border pieces.

032×4 Ledger & Curved Section Notes

Ledger (2×4 × 13')

  • Runs across full 13' width at transition line (~11.5' from back edge)
  • Screwed to ends of 2×6 field joists AND to 2×6 blocking between joist ends
  • Top of ledger should be flush with top of 2×6 joists (or ½" below — decking will bridge)
  • Provides bearing surface for 2×4 curved joists that fan toward pool

Curved Joists (2×4)

  • Fan outward from ledger toward pool edge
  • Center joist: ~6.5' long (longest)
  • Edge joists: ~2' long (shortest)
  • Cut ends at angle to follow pool curve (9–11 faceted pieces)
  • Space ~12–16" O.C., face-screw to ledger with 2.5" screws
  • Joist ends rest on ECO M pedestals near coping

💡 Pedestal Spacing for 11.5' Joists
For 2×6 joists at ~11.5' span, use 2 pedestals per joist (one near each end) plus a 3rd at mid-span on the center joist and rim joists. The 11.5' span is fine with 2 supports for foot traffic — it's a deck, not a dance floor.

05Summary: Build Day Cut Order

Stain everything first, then cut. Here's the order:

  1. Back rim: Cut one 14' 2×6 down to 13'. Offcut (1') → save for blocking.
  2. Side rims: Use two ~11.5' 2×6s at full length. NO cuts needed!
  3. Field joists: Use nine ~11.5' 2×6s at full length. NO cuts needed!
  4. Blocking: From offcut pile, cut 10 blocking pieces: nine at ~14.5", one at ~10.5".
  5. Ledger: Cut one 2×4 to 13' (or splice two shorter pieces with a 2' overlap and screws).
  6. Decking: Set aside 24 full 12' boards for main field. Cut 3 boards into 24× ~1' pieces for landing strip.
  7. Curved joists: Cut 2×4s to fan lengths: 1× ~6.5', ~6× 2'–3', 2× ~2'.
⚠️ Don't Forget
All cuts get stain on the fresh end grain BEFORE assembly. End grain drinks stain — hit it twice. The offcuts for blocking also need their ends sealed if they'll be exposed.
