🏗️ Design Philosophy: Boardwalk on Stone

This is NOT a traditional deck. It's a boardwalk-style floating platform that sits directly on flat stone or compacted gravel. No posts in the ground. No concrete footings. No ledger board attached to a house. The entire structure rests on Eurotec ECO M/L pedestals, making it fully freestanding, ultra-low profile, and removable if needed.

Key Heights: Main deck ~7" above grade · Curved pool edge ~5" above grade · Both sit well above coping

02Design Overview

A freestanding 22' × ~18' boardwalk deck with a curved front edge that follows the pool's shape. Two framing heights: main deck at ~7" and curved pool edge at ~5". No house connection. No posts in ground.

Structure at a Glance

  • Support: Eurotec ECO M/L pedestals on flat stone, compacted gravel, or combo base · NO posts in ground
  • Main deck framing: 2×6 joists (5.5") at 16" O.C., running front-to-back (perpendicular to back edge)
  • Curved edge framing: 2×4 joists (3.5") fanning from 2×4 ledger toward pool, following curve
  • Ledger: 2×4 ledger board across deck at transition line (~11.5' from back edge), connecting curved section to main deck
  • Main decking: 5/4×6 boards, run left→right (parallel to back edge)
  • Curved decking: 2×6 boards, run perpendicular (radial/front-to-back) to follow curve
  • Edge detail: Curved front overhangs pool coping by ~1", following pool radius

Height Breakdown

💡 Why Two Heights? The 2" height difference between main deck and curved edge is barely perceptible when standing on it, but it saves lumber (2×4 vs 2×6) on the section that only needs to span a short distance to the pool edge. The 2×4 ledger at the transition provides bearing for the curved joists and a clean attachment point.

03Framing Detail

Joist Layout (Main Deck — 2×6)

  • Outer rim joists: 2×6 perimeter band around full deck perimeter · Doubled 2×6 on all four sides (back, left, right, and front/ledger area)
  • Field joists: 2×6 at 16" O.C., running front-to-back (perpendicular to back edge)
  • Joists sit DIRECTLY on pedestals/stone: No beam underneath · Pedestals contact joist bottom directly
  • Ends: Joists are face-screwed to rim joists with 3" exterior screws · 2 per end
  • Ledger attachment: Main deck joists butt into 2×4 ledger at transition line · Ledger is face-screwed to rim or blocking

Curved Section (2×4)

  • 2×4 ledger: Runs full width of deck at transition line (~8' from back edge) · Screwed to main deck rim/ blocking · Provides bearing for curved joists
  • 2×4 joists: Fan outward from ledger toward pool edge · Lengths vary (longest at center, shortest at corners) · Spaced ~12–16" O.C.
  • Joist ends: Cut at angle to follow pool curve · Face-screwed to ledger with 2.5" screws · Ends rest on ECO M pedestals near coping
  • Curved rim: Optional 2×4 face board along curved front edge, screwed to joist ends for clean appearance

Support Pedestals

  • ECO M (1.4"–2.6"): Primary pedestal for lowest profile · Use where grade is already level · 19 on hand
  • ECO L (2.6"–5.1"): Use where ground slopes or needs more lift · 19 on hand · Adjustable by rotating threaded barrel
  • Placement: One pedestal under each joist intersection (approx 3 per joist) · Corners get precast concrete bases if using
  • Base: Flat stone pavers (most stable) OR compacted gravel (cheaper) OR combo — flat stone at corners/peaks, gravel in between

04Stain & Finishing

Stain BEFORE you build. Every piece gets all six faces sealed on sawhorses before it goes in the truck.

Product

  • BEHR PREMIUM #SC-125 Stonehedge — Solid Color Weatherproofing All-In-One Wood Stain
  • 5-gallon bucket (covers ~1,000–1,500 sq ft for two coats)
  • Color: Warm, earthy gray-brown that complements the wooded backyard

Application Method

⚠️ Critical: Stain ALL 6 Faces Before Assembly Every board gets top, bottom, and all four edges sealed before it leaves the sawhorses. End grain gets a double coat. This is your moisture barrier — don't skip it.
  1. Prep: Pressure wash all boards, apply wood brightener, let dry 48+ hours. No heavy sanding needed — the rough-sawn texture is part of the look.
  2. Setup: Stack boards on flat sawhorses with ½" gaps for air circulation. Label every piece with its planned position (e.g., "DECK-ROW-3", "CURVED-JOIST-5") using painter's tape and marker.
  3. End grain first: Brush a heavy coat on all cut ends — they drink stain. Let dry 30 min.
  4. Faces: Roll the broad surfaces with a ¼" nap roller for speed, then back-brush with a quality 3" brush to work stain into cracks and give that hand-finished look.
  5. Edges: Brush all four edges, especially where boards will butt together.
  6. Dry & flip: Let dry 4–6 hours, flip, repeat on the back face.
  7. Second coat (optional): After 24 hours, assess coverage. Solid stain usually needs one good coat, but high-traffic areas benefit from two thin coats vs one heavy one.
💡 Pro Tip: The "Wet Edge" On hot days, stain dries fast. Work in sections and maintain a wet edge — don't let a rolled section dry before you back-brush it, or you'll see lap marks. Morning shade is your friend.

05Build Sequence

Build in three phases: Prep → Frame → Deck. Each phase needs dry weather.

Phase 1: Site Prep

  1. Clear site of debris, roots, and any vegetation within deck footprint.
  2. Rough-grade the area with a rake and shovel. Goal: within ±1" of level across entire 22' × ~18' area.
  3. Spread 4–5 tons of crushed stone (¾" or smaller) OR place flat stone pavers at pedestal locations. Tamp well.
  4. Lay out pedestal locations with spray paint. Grid: one pedestal under each joist intersection, roughly 3 per joist line.
  5. Set precast concrete bases at left/right corners if using (accept 2× lumber directly).
  6. Install ECO M/L pedestals, adjusting height so tops are roughly level. Fine-tune during framing.

Phase 2: Main Deck Framing

  1. Cut 2×6 rim joists: back rim (spliced to 22'), transition rim (spliced to 22'), and two 11.5' side rims · Full length, no cuts on sides. Cut transition rim to accept 2×4 ledger later.
  2. Assemble outer rim on ground (or on pedestals if you have help): rectangle 22' × ~11.5' (back portion only). Check diagonal for square.
  3. Install field joists: 2×6 at 16" O.C., running front-to-back (perpendicular to back edge). Face-screw to rim with 3" exterior screws or structural screws (GRK RSS 5/16"×4"). Use 2 per end.
  4. Add diagonal bracing: Install 2×4 cross-bracing between field joists at corners and mid-span to prevent racking. Screw through joists into brace ends.
  5. Place assembled frame onto pedestals. Adjust pedestal heights so frame is level and at correct height (~7" total, leaving room for 1" decking).
  6. Install 2×4 ledger across transition line (where curved section begins). Screwed to rim joists and blocking. This is the bearing point for curved joists.
  7. Add blocking between joists near ledger and at mid-span for rigidity.

Phase 3: Curved Section Framing

  1. Cut 2×4 joists for curved section. Lengths vary: longest at center (~3–4'), shortest at corners (~2'). Cut ends at angle to follow pool curve.
  2. Space joists ~12–16" O.C. fanning from ledger toward pool. Face-screw to ledger with 2.5" exterior screws.
  3. Joist ends rest on ECO M pedestals near coping. Adjust pedestal height for ~5" total (3.5" joist + 1" decking = 4.5", pedestal makes up rest).
  4. Optional: Install curved 2×4 face board along front edge for clean appearance.

Phase 4: Decking

  1. Main deck: Start at back edge. Lay 5/4×6 boards left→right (parallel to back edge). Space 1/8"–3/16" apart for drainage (wider in humid Georgia climate). Screw with 2.5" exterior screws, two per joist.
  2. 22' challenge: 12' boards need extra foot. Options: (a) Frame in a 1' border with blocking and butt-join shorter pieces, (b) Use some boards at slight diagonal, (c) Accept a pieced section near back edge where it's less visible.
  3. Curved section: Lay boards perpendicular (radial/front-to-back), starting at ledger and working toward pool edge. Space 3/16" apart (2×6 boards expand more than 5/4). Boards follow the curve naturally when laid radially. Cut ends to match curved rim.
  4. Overhang: Curved decking should extend ~1" past coping for clean look. Verify from pool level that overhang is consistent.

Phase 5: Railings & Step

  1. Rail posts: Surface-mount short 6×6 posts (~3' total length) at corners and mid-points. Pre-drill, attach with ½" lag screws and washers into blocking below.
  2. Rails: Two 2×6 horizontal rails per section, 36" above deck surface. Butt joints at posts, no fancy joinery needed.
  3. Box step: Wide box step on back/outer edge. Single 7" rise using 2×6 frame and 5/4 tread. Size: at least 3' wide for comfortable access.

06Railings & Safety

  • Height: 36" above deck surface (standard residential)
  • Posts: 6×6 nominal (actual 5.5" × 5.5") · Short pieces (~3' total) · Surface-mounted with lag screws into blocking
  • Rails: 2×6 horizontal, two per bay · Top rail at 36", mid-rail at ~18" · Butt-joined at posts
  • No gate: Continuous railing all around — box step provides access

07Decking Notes

Direction Change

The decking changes direction at the 2×4 ledger:

  • Main deck: Boards run left→right (parallel to back edge, perpendicular to joists). This is the "normal" way decking runs.
  • Curved section: Boards run radial/perpendicular (front-to-back, following the curve). This looks intentional and follows the pool's geometry.
  • Visual transition: The 2×4 ledger creates a natural "break line" where the decking direction changes. This looks designed, not accidental.

22' Width Solution

⚠️ Your 5/4×6 boards are 12' — deck is 22' wide You need to cover an extra foot. Options (pick one or combine):

Option A: Frame in a 1' border along back edge with 2×4 blocking. Butt-join shorter decking pieces (cut from board ends) into this border. Least visible since it's against the back edge.

Option B:>/strong> Run every 4th or 5th board at a slight angle, gaining ~1" per board over 12'. Subtle and effective.

Option C: Accept a small pieced area. Cut 1' off some boards, use the offcuts to piece together the extra foot in one row.

10Quick Tips & Gotchas

💡 Start staining before you think you're ready Staining takes longer than building. If you have dry weather coming, start washing boards 2 days before you plan to stain. They need 48 hours to dry.
💡 Build a "test section" Before committing the whole deck, build a 4' × 4' test section with a few pedestals, one joist, and a couple decking boards. Verify heights, pedestal stability, and how it feels to walk on before scaling up.
⚠️ Don't over-tighten pedestal screws The ECO pedestals have a threaded barrel for height adjustment. Crank them snug, but don't reef on them — the plastic threads can strip if you're aggressive.
💡 Curved cuts don't need to be perfect The curved decking is forgiving. A faceted curve (9–11 straight cuts) looks fine and is much easier than a true radius cut. Sand the transitions smooth if needed.
💡 Leave the labels on during build The painter's tape labels you put on during staining? Leave them on until the piece is installed. You'll thank yourself when you're hunting for "CURVED-JOIST-3" in a pile of identical boards.
