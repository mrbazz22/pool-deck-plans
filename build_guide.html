🏗️ Design Philosophy: Two-Section Boardwalk on Stone

This is NOT a traditional deck. It's a boardwalk-style floating platform built as two independent sections that sit directly on flat stone or compacted gravel. No posts in the ground. No concrete footings. No ledger board. The entire structure rests on Eurotec ECO M/L pedestals, making it fully freestanding, ultra-low profile, and removable if needed.

Key Features: Two 11' × 11.5' sections side by side · 6" center seam · Double-row picture-frame border on every edge · Inner field boards run left→right · Border boards run perpendicular for a crafted, intentional look

Height: Main deck ~7" above grade · Sits well above coping

02Design Overview

Two 11' × 11.5' freestanding sections side by side, separated by a 6" center seam. Each section gets a double-row picture-frame border on all four sides. No curved front edge. No ledger. No house connection. No posts in ground.

Structure at a Glance

  • Two sections: Each 11' wide × 11.5' deep · Framed separately, then decked · Separated by 6" center seam
  • Support: Eurotec ECO M/L pedestals on flat stone or compacted gravel · NO posts in ground
  • Framing: 2×6 joists (5.5") at 16" O.C., running front-to-back (perpendicular to back edge) · Full 11.5' length = zero joist cuts
  • Rim joists: Doubled 2×6 on all four sides of each section · Back/front rims cut to 11' from 14' stock · Side rims use full ~11.5' stock
  • Border blocking: 2×6 blocking pieces every 16" O.C. under all border zones to support perpendicular border boards
  • Mid-span blocking: One row of 2×6 blocking at ~5.75' from back edge per section · Prevents joist twist
  • Center seam: 6" wide perpendicular strip running full 11.5' depth · Blocking underneath · Boards span the gap
  • Inner field: 9' 2" × 9' 6" per section · Field boards run left→right (parallel to back edge)
  • Border: Double-row picture-frame on all four sides of each section · 2 rows of 5/4×6 = 11" total border depth · Border boards run perpendicular to the edge they border
  • Decking direction: Field boards left→right · Border boards perpendicular · Seam boards perpendicular (front-to-back)

💡 Why Two Sections? Two 11' sections are easier to frame, move, and level than one massive 22'+ deck. Each section is manageable by two people. The 6" center seam is a visual accent that looks intentional — like a boardwalk expansion joint — and it simplifies the build. Picture-frame borders on every edge mean every board direction is deliberate.

03Framing Detail

Joist Layout (Per Section — 2×6)

  • Outer rim joists: Doubled 2×6 perimeter band on all four sides of each section · Back and front rims: 11' long, cut from 14' stock · Left and right rims: ~11.5' full length stock
  • Field joists: 2×6 at 16" O.C., running front-to-back (perpendicular to back edge) · Full 11.5' length — no cuts needed
  • Joists sit DIRECTLY on pedestals/stone: No beam underneath · Pedestals contact joist bottom directly
  • Ends: Joists are face-screwed to rim joists with 3" exterior screws · 2 per end
  • No ledger: Each section is self-contained · No transition line · No connection to house

Border Blocking (Critical for Picture-Frame)

  • Purpose: Perpendicular border boards need support underneath since they don't land on joists · 2×6 blocking provides nailing surface
  • Placement: Every 16" O.C. under all border zones — back edge, front edge, left side, right side of each section
  • Orientation: Blocking runs parallel to the rim it supports · Back/front blocking runs left→right · Side blocking runs front→back
  • Length: ~14–18" pieces, cut from 2×6 offcuts or extra stock
  • Attachment: Face-screwed to rim joists and adjacent field joists with 2.5" exterior screws

Mid-Span Blocking

  • One row of 2×6 blocking at ~5.75' from back edge of each section · Prevents 11.5' joists from twisting
  • One piece per bay between field joists · ~7–9 pieces per section
  • Cut from 2×6 offcuts · Face-screwed to joists

Center Seam Blocking

  • 6" wide gap between the two sections runs full 11.5' depth
  • 2×6 blocking underneath every 16" O.C. along the seam · Supports perpendicular seam boards
  • ~9–10 blocking pieces total · Cut from 2×6 scrap · Screwed to inner rim joists of both sections

Support Pedestals

  • ECO M (1.4"–2.6"): Primary pedestal for lowest profile · Use where grade is already level
  • ECO L (2.6"–5.1"): Use where ground slopes or needs more lift · Adjustable by rotating threaded barrel
  • Placement: Roughly 2–3 per joist under field joists · Additional pedestals under rim joists and blocking points · ~25–30 pedestals per section = ~55–65 total
  • Base: Flat stone pavers (most stable) OR compacted gravel (cheaper) OR combo — flat stone at corners/peaks, gravel in between

04Stain & Finishing

Stain BEFORE you build. Every piece gets all six faces sealed on sawhorses before it goes in the truck.

Product

  • BEHR PREMIUM #SC-125 Stonehedge — Solid Color Weatherproofing All-In-One Wood Stain
  • 5-gallon bucket (covers ~1,000–1,500 sq ft for two coats)
  • Color: Warm, earthy gray-brown that complements the wooded backyard

Application Method

⚠️ Critical: Stain ALL 6 Faces Before Assembly Every board gets top, bottom, and all four edges sealed before it leaves the sawhorses. End grain gets a double coat. This is your moisture barrier — don't skip it.
  1. Prep: Pressure wash all boards, apply wood brightener, let dry 48+ hours. No heavy sanding needed — the rough-sawn texture is part of the look.
  2. Setup: Stack boards on flat sawhorses with ½" gaps for air circulation. Label every piece with its planned position (e.g., "SEC-A-FIELD-3", "SEC-A-BORDER-L1") using painter's tape and marker.
  3. End grain first: Brush a heavy coat on all cut ends — they drink stain. Let dry 30 min.
  4. Faces: Roll the broad surfaces with a ¼" nap roller for speed, then back-brush with a quality 3" brush to work stain into cracks and give that hand-finished look.
  5. Edges: Brush all four edges, especially where boards will butt together.
  6. Dry & flip: Let dry 4–6 hours, flip, repeat on the back face.
  7. Second coat (optional): After 24 hours, assess coverage. Solid stain usually needs one good coat, but high-traffic areas benefit from two thin coats vs one heavy one.
💡 Pro Tip: The "Wet Edge" On hot days, stain dries fast. Work in sections and maintain a wet edge — don't let a rolled section dry before you back-brush it, or you'll see lap marks. Morning shade is your friend.

05Build Sequence

Build in phases: Prep → Frame → Block → Deck. Each phase needs dry weather.

Phase 1: Site Prep

  1. Clear site of debris, roots, and any vegetation within deck footprint (~23' × 12').
  2. Rough-grade the area with a rake and shovel. Goal: within ±1" of level across entire area.
  3. Mark out two 11' × 11.5' rectangles side by side with 6" gap between them. Use string lines and spray paint.
  4. Spread 4–5 tons of crushed stone (¾" or smaller) OR place flat stone pavers at pedestal locations. Tamp well.
  5. Lay out pedestal locations with spray paint. Grid: 2–3 pedestals per joist, plus extras under rims and blocking.
  6. Install ECO M/L pedestals, adjusting height so tops are roughly level. Fine-tune during framing.

Phase 2: Frame Two Sections

  1. Cut 2×6 back/front rims: cut four 14' boards to 11' each (2 per section). Offcuts (~3') save for blocking.
  2. Assemble Section A outer rim on ground: rectangle 11' × 11.5' with doubled 2×6 on all sides. Check diagonal for square.
  3. Install Section A field joists: 2×6 at 16" O.C., running front-to-back. Face-screw to rim with 3" exterior screws or structural screws. Use 2 per end.
  4. Repeat for Section B.
  5. Place assembled frames onto pedestals. Adjust pedestal heights so frames are level and at correct height (~7" total, leaving room for 1" decking). Check that the 6" seam gap is consistent.
  6. Add diagonal bracing inside each section: Install 2×4 or 2×6 cross-bracing between field joists at corners to prevent racking. Screw through joists into brace ends.

Phase 3: Blocking

  1. Mid-span blocking: Install one row of 2×6 blocking at ~5.75' from back edge in each section. One piece per bay between field joists.
  2. Border blocking: Install 2×6 pieces every 16" O.C. under all border zones of each section — back edge, front edge, left side, right side. This is critical for supporting perpendicular border boards.
  3. Center seam blocking: Install 2×6 blocking every 16" O.C. underneath the 6" seam gap. Screwed to inner rim joists of both sections.
  4. Apply butyl joist tape to tops of all joists, rims, and blocking before decking.

Phase 4: Decking — Inner Field

  1. Start at back edge of each section. Lay 5/4×6 field boards left→right (parallel to back edge), working toward the front.
  2. Each field board is cut to ~9' 6" (from 12' stock). The ~2' 6" offcut becomes border stock.
  3. Space boards ⅛"–3/16" apart for drainage. Screw with 2.5" exterior screws, two per joist.
  4. Stop each section's field decking 11" from all edges — leave room for the double border.
  5. Verify field boards end cleanly at the border line. Trim if needed before installing borders.

Phase 5: Decking — Picture-Frame Borders

  1. Install outer border row first on all four sides of each section. Boards run perpendicular to the edge they border.
  2. Back/front borders: boards run front-to-back, butted against the field edge. Each piece is ~11" long (spanning the full border depth). Space ⅛" apart.
  3. Side borders: boards run left→right, butted against the field edge. Each piece ~11" long.
  4. Install inner border row inside the outer row, same perpendicular direction. This creates the double-row picture-frame effect.
  5. Corner details: Butt joints at corners work fine. For a cleaner look, miter outer row corners at 45°.
  6. Pre-drill all border boards — short pieces split easily.

Phase 6: Decking — Center Seam

  1. Lay 5/4×6 boards across the 6" seam gap, running front-to-back (perpendicular to the seam).
  2. Each piece is ~6" long, cut to span the gap. Space ⅛" apart.
  3. Screw into seam blocking underneath. ~24–26 pieces total.
  4. The seam boards create a visual "expansion joint" that looks intentional.

Phase 7: Railings & Step

  1. Rail posts: Surface-mount short 6×6 posts (~3' total length) at outer corners and mid-points of the combined deck. Pre-drill, attach with ½" lag screws and washers into blocking below.
  2. Rails: Two 2×6 horizontal rails per section, 36" above deck surface. Butt joints at posts.
  3. Box step: Wide box step on back/outer edge. Single 7" rise using 2×6 frame and 5/4 tread. Size: at least 3' wide for comfortable access.

06Railings & Safety

  • Height: 36" above deck surface (standard residential)
  • Posts: 6×6 nominal (actual 5.5" × 5.5") · Short pieces (~3' total) · Surface-mounted with lag screws into blocking
  • Rails: 2×6 horizontal, two per bay · Top rail at 36", mid-rail at ~18" · Butt-joined at posts
  • No gate: Continuous railing around outer perimeter — box step provides access

07Decking Notes

Picture-Frame Double Border

The double-row picture-frame border is the signature detail of this deck. Here's how it works:

  • Field boards run left→right (parallel to back edge), covering the 9' 2" × 9' 6" inner area of each section
  • Border boards run perpendicular to the edge they border · Back/front borders run front-to-back · Side borders run left→-right
  • Two rows deep: Each border zone is 11" deep (2 rows of 5/4×6 at ~5.5" wide each)
  • Visual effect: The perpendicular border creates a crisp frame around the field, like a picture frame. The double row adds visual weight and a crafted, intentional look.
  • Structural benefit: Perpendicular borders interlock with the field edge, reducing lateral movement and giving the deck edge more rigidity

Border Blocking Is Essential

⚠️ Do Not Skip Border Blocking Perpendicular border boards only touch the rim joist at one end. Without blocking underneath every 16", the free end of each short border piece will flex, squeak, and eventually crack. The 2×6 blocking pieces are cut from offcuts — there's no reason to skip this step.

Center Seam

The 6" center seam between sections is both practical and visual:

  • Practical: Allows each section to expand/contract independently · Simplifies framing — no need to splice or join two sections
  • Visual: Short perpendicular boards across the seam look like an expansion joint — common on boardwalks and docks
  • Drainage: The gap allows water to drain through instead of pooling

Cut Strategy for 5/4×6

💡 Optimize Your Cuts Each 12' board yields one 9' 6" field board + one ~2' 6" offcut. Each offcut yields ~2 border pieces (~11" each). Plan your cuts so the offcuts are clean and straight — wavy or cracked ends should be trimmed before cutting border stock.

10Quick Tips & Gotchas

💡 Start staining before you think you're ready Staining takes longer than building. If you have dry weather coming, start washing boards 2 days before you plan to stain. They need 48 hours to dry.
💡 Build a "test section" Before committing both sections, build a 4' × 4' test section with a few pedestals, one joist, and a couple decking boards. Verify heights, pedestal stability, and how it feels to walk on before scaling up.
⚠️ Don't over-tighten pedestal screws The ECO pedestals have a threaded barrel for height adjustment. Crank them snug, but don't reef on them — the plastic threads can strip if you're aggressive.
💡 Picture-frame corners look best mitered For the outer border row, miter the corners at 45° for a clean, finished look. The inner border row can butt directly against the outer row — no need to miter both rows.
💡 Leave the labels on during build The painter's tape labels you put on during staining? Leave them on until the piece is installed. You'll thank yourself when you're hunting for "SEC-A-BORDER-L3" in a pile of identical boards.
⚠️ Check the 6" seam gap before decking Verify the center seam is consistently 6" before you cut seam boards. If one end is 5" and the other is 7", your seam pieces won't fit. Adjust framing or shim as needed.
